# Chapter 1: Introduction to the University Academic Portal

## 1.1 Background of the Current System

Universities depend on accurate and timely academic information to manage admission, enrolment, grading, fee collection, timetable administration, attendance monitoring, and communication with students and staff. For many years, these activities were handled through paper forms, handwritten registers, spreadsheets, and disconnected communication channels. Although such methods were acceptable when student numbers were lower and academic operations were less complex, they are increasingly unsuitable for modern higher education environments because they are slow, repetitive, and prone to human error (MasterSoft, 2024; Team, 2024).

In many institutions, student registration data is still entered manually, course choices are processed through forms or spreadsheets, grades are submitted through offline documents, and fee records are maintained separately by finance staff. Timetables are often prepared using spreadsheet tools, while attendance is taken in paper registers and later compiled for reporting. Communication is also frequently fragmented across notice boards, e-mail, and informal channels. As a result, staff may duplicate work, students may need to visit offices physically to confirm information, and academic records may be difficult to update consistently or report accurately (Go, 2025; HEIapply, 2023; Tennyson, 2025).

Some universities have introduced partial digital solutions, such as using spreadsheets for timetable preparation or e-mail for announcements. However, these approaches do not fully solve the core problem because they remain disconnected from each other. Information must still be transferred between systems manually, increasing the likelihood of inconsistency, delay, and data duplication. This situation creates a strong need for an integrated academic portal that can centralise key academic processes within a single, secure, web-based platform (Kumar et al., 2024; University, 2025).

The University Academic Portal proposed in this project responds to that need. The system is intended to provide a single environment through which students, teachers, and administrative staff can access and manage academic information more efficiently. By replacing fragmented and manual procedures with an integrated portal, the project aims to improve service delivery, reduce administrative workload, and support faster and more reliable academic decision-making.

## 1.2 Current Business Process

### 1.2.1 Student Registration Process

In the current manual process, new student information is collected using paper forms or basic office documents. Administrative staff then enter personal details, contact information, academic background, and supporting document information into spreadsheets or paper files. Once the record has been created, the university assigns a student identifier and stores the record for academic and administrative use (CAMBRIDGE, 2025).

### 1.2.2 Course Registration Process

Students select courses through manual registration forms or through a staff-assisted process. Staff check the selected courses, review timetable compatibility, and record the approved choices in spreadsheets or internal logs. These records are later used to prepare class lists, confirm enrolment status, and support timetable planning (HEIapply, 2023).

### 1.2.3 Grade Submission Process

Teachers record and submit student grades using paper forms, spreadsheets, or offline files. Administrative staff then re-enter the data into academic records systems or spreadsheets for storage and reporting. This process is also used to support result publication and performance tracking, but the workflow depends heavily on repeated manual input (Tennyson, 2025).

### 1.2.4 Fee Payment Process

Students commonly pay tuition or academic fees through the finance office using cash, bank transfer, or other offline methods. Staff record payment status in spreadsheets, ledgers, or separate finance files. Students often need to contact the office directly to verify whether a payment has been received and processed.

### 1.2.5 Timetable Management Process

Timetables for courses, subjects, rooms, and teachers are often prepared manually using spreadsheets or printed schedules. When changes occur, staff must update the information by hand and redistribute revised schedules to the relevant stakeholders. This makes timetable administration time-consuming and increases the risk of clashes and outdated information being circulated (shyfter, 2023).

### 1.2.6 Attendance Process

Attendance is usually recorded by teachers during class using paper registers or manual lists. These attendance records are later collected and compiled for reporting and administrative follow-up. In large classes, this process becomes difficult to manage consistently and can delay the identification of low-attendance cases (Gradelink, 2016).

### 1.2.7 Communication Process

Academic communication often relies on notice boards, printed notices, e-mail messages, or informal announcements. As these channels are not centralised, students may miss important updates relating to schedules, academic deadlines, examinations, fee reminders, or general notices. Staff must also spend additional time repeating or redistributing information.

## 1.3 Current Business Issues (Manual System Issues)

### 1.3.1 Student Registration Process Issue

Manual student registration is slow and vulnerable to data entry errors, duplication, and incomplete records. When information is stored across paper files and spreadsheets, it becomes harder to maintain consistency across academic, administrative, and financial records (Staff, 2024).

### 1.3.2 Course Registration Process Issue

Manual course registration delays enrolment confirmation and increases the possibility of invalid selections, duplicate records, and timetable clashes. Because the process depends on staff checking and updating records manually, course administration becomes inefficient and difficult to scale (Cox, 2025).

### 1.3.3 Grade Submission Process Issue

When grades are first recorded on paper or offline files and then re-entered into academic records, the process becomes slower and more error-prone. It also delays grade publication, progress monitoring, and academic reporting, especially when many students and modules are involved (Staff, 2024).

### 1.3.4 Fee Payment Process Issue

Manual fee tracking creates risks of inaccurate payment records, delayed confirmation, and poor visibility for both students and staff. Students may need to visit the finance office physically, while administrators must spend additional effort checking payment status and preparing reports (Faurie, 2025).

### 1.3.5 Timetable Management Process Issue

Preparing and updating timetables manually is difficult because class schedules, teacher availability, and room allocation must all be coordinated carefully. Where timetable information is handled through spreadsheets or paper, conflicts are harder to detect and resolve, and schedule updates may not reach users quickly enough (timetablemaster, 2025).

### 1.3.6 Attendance Process Issue

Attendance tracking by hand is labour-intensive and difficult to audit. Errors in attendance records may affect reporting accuracy, academic monitoring, and intervention for at-risk students. In addition, manual attendance collection makes it harder to identify low-attendance trends early enough for corrective action.

### 1.3.7 Communication Process Issue

When academic communication is spread across notice boards, printed notices, and e-mail, students and staff do not have a single reliable place to obtain updates. This increases the risk of missed announcements, delayed responses, and inconsistent information.

### 1.3.8 Overall Problem Statement

The overall problem is that the current academic administration process is fragmented across manual and partially digital methods, resulting in slow processing, duplicated effort, inconsistent records, and limited visibility for users. As student numbers, academic data, and administrative demands increase, these weaknesses become more serious. Therefore, there is a clear need for a secure and integrated University Academic Portal that centralises core academic services and provides accurate, timely, and accessible information for students, teachers, and administrative staff.

## 1.4 SWOT Analysis of the Current System

### Strengths

The existing manual system has some limited strengths. It is familiar to staff, requires little technical training, and can continue operating without complete dependence on network connectivity or specialised software. It also supports direct human interaction between students and staff during administrative processes.

### Weaknesses

The system has significant weaknesses. It is slow, repetitive, and highly dependent on manual data entry. Paper files and spreadsheets increase the risk of duplication, loss of records, inconsistent updates, and reporting delays. The current approach also makes monitoring student progress, fee status, attendance, and communication more difficult than necessary.

### Opportunities

There is a strong opportunity to improve academic administration through digital transformation. A web-based academic portal can centralise data, improve accessibility, support automated workflows, and provide better reporting for operational and academic decision-making. It can also improve user experience for students by offering easier access to academic services from one platform.

### Threats

The current manual approach is threatened by growing student populations, higher expectations for digital services, rising administrative workload, and competition from institutions that already provide integrated online academic systems. In addition, paper-based and fragmented systems are more vulnerable to record loss, delays, and poor service quality over time.

## 1.5 Proposed System Scope

The proposed University Academic Portal is a web-based academic management system designed to replace fragmented manual and spreadsheet-based processes with an integrated digital platform. The system focuses on the core academic functions identified in the proposal: student registration, course registration, grade management, fee payment, timetable management, attendance tracking, and communication. The portal is intended to support three main user groups: students, teachers, and administrative staff.

The scope of the project is limited to academic and administrative workflows directly related to these core functions. The portal is not intended to replace every university system. Instead, it provides a focused academic platform that improves efficiency, record accuracy, accessibility, and communication. The implemented system also includes supporting capabilities, such as notifications, approval workflows, and related academic tools, where these features strengthen the main project objectives.

### 1.5.1 Student Registration Process

The portal digitises student registration through a staff-administered workflow in which student personal details, academic background, and supporting documents are entered and stored electronically. The system automatically generates unique student numbers, maintains records in a secure database, and allows authorised staff to review and update information when required. This replaces paper-based registration and reduces duplication and data entry errors.

### 1.5.2 Course Registration Process

The portal enables students to request enrolment in courses online. The system validates requests, prevents duplicate enrolment, and supports timetable conflict checking before approval. Administrative staff can review and manage enrolment requests, while teachers can view the students assigned to their courses. This provides a more accurate and efficient alternative to spreadsheet-based course administration.

### 1.5.3 Grade Submission Process

The portal allows teachers to submit grades electronically and supports staff review of grade records before final academic visibility. It also supports the display of approved grades and GPA-related information for students. By replacing manual grade handling with a digital workflow, the system improves timeliness, traceability, and record accuracy.

### 1.5.4 Fee Payment Process

The portal supports digital fee management by allowing students to view fee records and submit payments through the system. Administrative staff can monitor payment status, review submissions, issue receipts, and track overdue fees. This reduces reliance on manual finance checking and improves visibility for both students and administrators.

### 1.5.5 Timetable Management Process

The portal provides a central timetable management function through which staff can create, update, and manage schedules for courses, subjects, rooms, and teachers. Conflict checking helps reduce overlapping timetable entries, while students and teachers can access updated schedules online. This improves timetable accuracy and reduces the risks associated with manually maintained schedules.

### 1.5.6 Attendance Tracking Process

The portal enables teachers to record student attendance electronically for their assigned classes. Attendance records are stored centrally and can be used to generate reports, monitor attendance patterns, and identify low-attendance cases. This improves reporting efficiency and reduces the administrative effort required by paper-based attendance systems.

### 1.5.7 Communication and Notifications

The portal provides a centralised communication mechanism through announcements, internal messages, and system notifications. Academic and administrative updates can therefore be shared more consistently, helping students and staff to access current information from one system rather than depending on multiple disconnected channels.

### 1.5.8 Significance of the Project

This project is significant because it addresses a practical and recurring problem in academic administration. For students, the portal improves access to academic services, reduces delays, and provides clearer visibility of enrolment status, grades, fees, timetables, and announcements. For teachers, it supports more structured management of grades, attendance, and communication. For administrative staff, it reduces repetitive manual work and improves the quality and availability of academic records.

From an academic perspective, the project is also significant because it demonstrates the design and development of a realistic multi-user information system using modern web technologies. The project therefore has value both as a practical solution to an identified university problem and as a final-year computing project that applies software engineering principles, database design, user-centred design, testing, and evaluation in an integrated way.

## 1.6 Aim and Objectives of the Project

### Aim

The aim of this project is to design, develop, and evaluate a web-based University Academic Portal using Laravel, Vue.js, and MySQL to manage student registration, course enrolment, grades, fee payment, timetable access, attendance tracking, and academic communication within a single integrated system.

### Objectives

1. To analyse the existing manual and partially digital academic processes and identify the functional and non-functional requirements of the proposed portal.
2. To design the University Academic Portal using appropriate system architecture, database structures, UML models, and interface prototypes.
3. To implement the portal iteratively in planned timeboxes, covering the core academic workflows of registration, enrolment, grades, fees, timetable, attendance, and communication.
4. To test and refine the system through functional and usability testing in order to confirm correctness, reliability, and ease of use.
5. To evaluate the completed system against the project aim, proposal scope, and comparable academic portals, and to prepare supporting deployment, training, and documentation materials.
